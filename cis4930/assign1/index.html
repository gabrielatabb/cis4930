History of Peru

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the timeline of Peru's history, from pre-columbian civilizations to modern times.
  • Identify ley periods in Peru's history, such as the Inca Empire, Spanish colonization, independence, and the Republican era.
  • Analyze the political, economic, and social changes that occurred during the spanish conquest of the Inca Empire

Learning Content

Peru’s history stretches back thousands of years, with early civilizations flourishing long before the Inca Empire rose to power. Cultures like the Caral, Nazca, and Moche developed advanced societies, each leaving behind impressive achievements. The Nazca created giant geoglyphs in the desert, while the Moche were masterful artists and builders. These early civilizations laid the groundwork for the development of the Inca Empire, which emerged in the Andes during the 1400s and became the largest empire in pre-Columbian America.

The Inca Empire was known for its remarkable engineering and agricultural techniques. The Incas constructed cities like Machu Picchu, which still stands as a symbol of their ingenuity. They built an extensive road network to connect their empire, stretching over 24,000 miles, and used terrace farming to grow crops in the challenging mountainous terrain. The Inca people also practiced a unique system of record-keeping using quipus, knotted strings that conveyed information. Their society thrived until the arrival of Spanish conquistadors in the 16th century.

In 1532, the Spanish, led by Francisco Pizarro, conquered the Inca Empire, marking the beginning of colonial rule in Peru. The Spanish established the Viceroyalty of Peru, turning the region into a major center of their global empire. Silver mines, especially in Potosí, became a source of immense wealth for Spain but brought hardship to indigenous populations, who were forced into labor. Spanish colonization introduced Christianity, the Spanish language, and European customs, but many indigenous traditions and practices endured, blending with the new culture to create a unique identity.

Today, Peru is a country where ancient history and modern life coexist. The influence of the Inca and pre-Inca civilizations remains evident in its archaeological sites, traditional clothing, and indigenous languages like Quechua and Aymara. Festivals and cultural celebrations, such as Inti Raymi, honor the country’s rich heritage. At the same time, Peru has grown into a modern nation known for its vibrant cities, diverse landscapes, and culinary traditions like ceviche. The story of Peru is a testament to the resilience and creativity of its people throughout history.
